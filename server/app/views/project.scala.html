About

The Covid-19 Mortality dashboard is a collaborative effort by researchers at the Leverhulme Centre for Demographic Science at the University of Oxford and the Interdisciplinary Centre on Population Dynamics at the University of Southern Denmark. The dashboard visualizes the main findings from the academic paper entitled 'Quantifying impacts of the COVID-19 pandemic through life expectancy losses'. The visualizations show period life expectancy levels in 2020 for 29 countries, covering Europe, the US and Chile, for which high quality age- and sex-disaggregated data on all-cause mortality for all of 2020 had been released by June 2021. To contextualize the magnitudes of life expectancy changes observed during the pandemic in 2020, we also provide estimates of recent levels and trends in life expectancy in these countries for 2015 and 2019. Code for both the dashboard and the accompanying academic article can be found here. Programming for the dashboard was undertaken by Ian Knowles, with input from Ridhi Kashyap, Charles Rahal, José Manuel Aburto, Jonas Schöley, Ilya Kashnitsky, Luyin Zhang, Trifon Missov, Jennifer Dowd, and Melinda Mills. Be sure to follow the LCDS and CPop on Twitter for all of the latest research updates.

Indicators

Our study calculates and reports period life expectancy levels to highlight the impacts of the pandemic on population health through the use of this indicator. We report life expectancy at birth (age 0) and at other ages (0, 10, 20, etc), as well as changes in period life expectancy observed between 2015, 2019 and 2020. Life expectancy at birth is the most widely-used metric of population health and longevity. The most commonly-reported version of life expectancy is period life expectancy, which is also what we report.

Period life expectancy refers to the average number of years a hypothetical cohort of newborns would live if they were to experience the death rates observed in a given period throughout their lifespan. This indicator is thus often referred to as period life expectancy, as it simulates and summarizes the implications of a set of mortality rates observed in a given period, such as a calendar year. The period life expectancy indicator does not describe how long on average an actual cohort – i.e. a group of people actually born in a year – would live, although that metric does exist, and is called a cohort life expectancy. However, waiting until every member of a cohort dies in order to calculate cohort life expectancy would require us to wait a long time. To enable more timely measurements of current conditions, period life expectancy is thus calculated and reported. Period life expectancy should not however be interpreted as a projection or forecast of any individual’s lifespan.

The key advantage of period life expectancy is that it is the preferred indicator for comparisons over time and across countries with populations of different sizes and age structures. Life expectancy in this form can also be calculated as conditional on surviving to a given age (e.g. 60, as defaulted to on the landing page for this dashboard), where it refers to remaining life expectancy from age 60. The study of life expectancy in the context of the COVID-19 pandemic matters because it enables us to compare the cumulative impacts of the pandemic against recent trends across different countries using a standardized indicator that is routinely monitored to capture differences in mortality.

Data

Our life expectancy calculations rely on data from different sources. All-cause death counts were retrieved from the Short Term Mortality Fluctuations data series within the Human Mortality Database. Additional all-cause mortality data were retrieved for the USA from the CDC and for England & Wales from the ONS to supplement the Short Term Mortality Fluctuations data. The Short Term Mortality Fluctuations provides high-quality weekly death counts for 38 countries (June 2021) in both a harmonized and original format, but the completeness of these data varies by country. We processed the original input files and selected countries for further analysis. Countries with weeks missing from the 2020 death tabulation or with coarse age grouping schemes were dropped from the analysis. Death counts for the USA and England & Wales across the years 2015 to 2019 were only reported in extremely coarse age groups by the STMF, but were available in fine grouping for 2020. For both countries we therefore utilized age-specific annual death counts as reported by the CDC and the ONS for the years prior to 2020.
